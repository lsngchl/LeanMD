# Sobolev Smoothing for the Generalized Radon Transform

Let \(m\ge2\), let \(d\ge1\) be an integer, let \(U,V\subset(\mathbb R^d)^m\) be open, and let \(\Phi\in C^\infty(U\times V;\mathbb R)\).
Let \(\chi\in C_c^\infty(U\times V)\).
Assume that \(\nabla_Y\Phi\ne0\) on \(\operatorname{spt}\chi\) and that, for every \(r\in\mathbb R\), the conormal canonical relation \(C_r\) of \(\Phi(X,Y)=r\) is locally a canonical graph at every point arising from \((X,Y)\in\operatorname{spt}\chi\).
For \(f\in C_c^\infty(V)\), define
\[
T_r^\chi f(X)
:=
\int_{\{Y\in V:\Phi(X,Y)=r\}}
f(Y)\chi(X,Y)\,d\sigma_{X,r}(Y).
\]

## Proposition

For every \(s\in\mathbb R\), the operator \(T_r^\chi\) extends to a bounded map
\[
T_r^\chi:H^s(V)\longrightarrow H^{s+(md-1)/2}(U).
\]
More precisely, for every compact interval \(I\subset\mathbb R\), there is a constant \(C_{s,\chi,I}\) such that
\[
\|T_r^\chi f\|_{H^{s+(md-1)/2}(U)}
\le
C_{s,\chi,I}\|f\|_{H^s(V)},
\qquad r\in I.
\tag{SM}
\]
Thus averaging \(f\) over the level hypersurfaces \(\Phi(X,Y)=r\) produces an output with \((md-1)/2\) more derivatives of \(L^2\)-Sobolev regularity than the input.

## Proof

The assumption \(\nabla_Y\Phi\ne0\) makes \(\{Y\in V:\Phi(X,Y)=r\}\) a smooth hypersurface wherever the cutoff is nonzero.

### The kernel and its canonical relation

By the coarea formula, the Schwartz kernel of \(T_r^\chi\) with respect to Lebesgue measure in \(Y\) is
\[
K_r(X,Y)=a(X,Y)\,\delta(\Phi(X,Y)-r),
\]
where
\[
a(X,Y):=\chi(X,Y)|\nabla_Y\Phi(X,Y)|
\]
belongs to \(C_c^\infty(U\times V)\).
Using the Fourier representation of the delta distribution gives
\[
K_r(X,Y)
=
\frac1{2\pi}
\int_{\mathbb R}
e^{i\tau(\Phi(X,Y)-r)}a(X,Y)\,d\tau.
\]
The normal covectors generated by this oscillatory phase are
\[
\xi=\tau\nabla_X\Phi(X,Y),
\qquad
\eta=-\tau\nabla_Y\Phi(X,Y),
\]
so the Fourier integral operator \(T_r^\chi\) has canonical relation \(C_r\).

### The Fourier integral order

The kernel above is an oscillatory integral on the \(2md\)-dimensional space \(U\times V\), with one homogeneous phase variable \(\tau\) and an amplitude of symbol order zero.
For an oscillatory integral on a base of dimension \(q\), with \(N\) phase variables and amplitude order \(p\), the corresponding Fourier integral order is
\[
p+\frac N2-\frac q4.
\]
Here \(p=0\), \(N=1\), and \(q=2md\), so
\[
\operatorname{ord}(T_r^\chi)
=
0+\frac12-\frac{2md}{4}
=
-\frac{md-1}{2}.
\tag{ORD}
\]

### Application of the Sobolev mapping theorem

The compact support of \(\chi\) is covered by finitely many coordinate patches on each of which \(C_r\) is the graph of a canonical transformation.
A partition of unity decomposes \(T_r^\chi\) into a finite sum of operators associated with these graph patches.
For each localized piece, \(U\) and \(V\) have the same dimension \(md\), its canonical relation is nondegenerate, and its Fourier integral order is \(-(md-1)/2\) by \((\mathrm{ORD})\).
Taking
\[
d_1=d_2=md,
\qquad
\mu=-\frac{md-1}{2}
\]
in the [Sobolev mapping theorem](fio_sobolev_theorem/fio_sobolev_theorem.md "why") gives exactly \((\mathrm{SM})\) for that piece.
Summing the finitely many localized estimates proves \((\mathrm{SM})\) for \(T_r^\chi\).

Because the phase depends smoothly on \(r\) and the amplitude is fixed, the resulting estimates are uniform when \(r\) ranges over a sufficiently small compact interval.
Covering a fixed compact interval \(I\) by finitely many such intervals makes the constant uniform for every \(r\in I\).
This proves the proposition. \(\square\)
